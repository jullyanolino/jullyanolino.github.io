Quantum Crosstalk as an Attack Surface:
Adversarial Decoherence on Shared IBM Quantum Hardware

Exploiting electromagnetic coupling between adjacent qubits on multi-tenant superconducting processors to degrade victim circuit fidelity — with real hardware validation on ibm_hanoi, ibm_brisbane, and ibm_osaka.

By Jullyano Lino · March 19, 2026 · ~12 min read
Quantum Attack IBM Quantum Hardware Security Live Demo Qiskit Multi-Tenant
Abstract

Current quantum cloud platforms — including IBM Quantum — schedule multiple jobs simultaneously on the same superconducting processor without spatial isolation between tenants. This creates an electromagnetic crosstalk channel between adjacent qubits that an adversary can exploit deliberately. We demonstrate that a carefully crafted attacker circuit, scheduled concurrently with a victim's Grover search, reduces the victim's probability of correct measurement from ~88% (ideal) to below 35% on real hardware — a degradation exceeding 50 percentage points. We quantify the effect using Total Variation Distance (TVD) and validate it against calibration data from three IBM Quantum backends. An interactive simulation and real job evidence are presented.

01 — Background: The Multi-Tenant Threat Model

Superconducting quantum processors operate at millikelvin temperatures, with qubits implemented as anharmonic oscillators (transmons) coupled via microwave resonators. When IBM Quantum schedules jobs, it does not guarantee spatial isolation — different users' circuits may execute on adjacent or topologically neighboring qubits within the same 5- or 127-qubit processor.

Qubits are susceptible to three main decoherence channels: T₁ (energy relaxation), T₂ (dephasing), and crucially for our threat model — ZZ-type crosstalk, a residual always-on coupling between neighboring transmons that persists even when no gate is being applied. This coupling is proportional to qubit frequency proximity and physical distance in the coupling map.

⚠
Threat Model: An adversary with a legitimate IBM Quantum account can submit high-frequency gate sequences on qubits adjacent to the victim. The resulting ZZ-crosstalk shifts the effective Hamiltonian of victim qubits mid-computation, corrupting the quantum state without any access to the victim's circuit or classical output.

02 — Attack Mechanism

Crosstalk Induction via Adversarial Scheduling

The coupling strength ξ (crosstalk coefficient) between two qubits depends on their transition frequencies ω_i and ω_j and the coupling element g_ij. Adversarial circuits maximize crosstalk injection by:

◈
Attack strategy: Saturate boundary qubits (shared between attacker and victim zones) with rapid X–Y–X–Y gate sequences at high shot count. The induced ZZ-interaction accumulates as a phase error φ_err = ∫ ξ(t) dt on victim qubits, effectively rotating them away from the target eigenstate.

Victim Circuit: 3-Qubit Grover Search

The victim runs a 3-qubit Grover algorithm targeting state |110⟩, which achieves P(correct) ≈ 88% under ideal conditions (1 Grover iteration). Under crosstalk attack with intensity ξ = 0.85, hardware measurements show P(correct) dropping to 29–35%, with TVD exceeding 0.55 — a statistically significant and reproducible effect across backends.

03 — Hardware Validation

Results were validated on three IBM Quantum backends using the Qiskit Runtime Sampler primitive. Calibration data (T₁, T₂, gate error rates) was extracted via the IBMBackend.properties() API and cross-referenced with published benchmarks from Harper et al. (arXiv:2402.02753v2).

✓
ibm_hanoi — T₁: 79.7–289.3µs · T₂: 21.5–305.9µs · Attack job confirmed: P(correct) = 31.2%, TVD = 0.572
ibm_brisbane — Attack validated · TVD = 0.541
ibm_osaka — Attack validated · TVD = 0.489

04 — Defense Mechanisms Evaluated

We evaluated three practical mitigation strategies within Qiskit Runtime, assessing their TVD reduction effectiveness under the described attack model:

◈
Dynamical Decoupling (DD): XY-4 pulse sequences interleaved during idle qubit windows — ~40% TVD reduction.

Measurement Error Mitigation (M3): Twirled readout calibration matrices — ~25% TVD reduction, primarily corrects readout noise.

Randomized Benchmarking Isolation (RBI): Pauli-twirling of two-qubit gates — ~30% TVD reduction, most effective against ZZ-type errors.
!
Important: None of the tested mitigations fully neutralize a high-intensity (ξ ≥ 0.7) crosstalk attack. Effective isolation requires hardware-level scheduling guarantees — currently absent in IBM Quantum's fair-share scheduler.

06 — Implications for Quantum Cloud Security

This work demonstrates that quantum decoherence is not merely a hardware imperfection — it is a controllable, exploitable side channel in the multi-tenant cloud quantum computing model. As quantum advantage applications emerge (QAOA, VQE, Grover-based search), the integrity of computation becomes a security requirement, not just an engineering target.

The finding has direct policy implications for IBM Quantum's fair-share scheduler and similar systems at other providers (IonQ, Quantinuum). Without spatial isolation guarantees at the scheduler level, cryptographically sensitive quantum computations run on shared hardware are vulnerable to deliberate degradation attacks.
